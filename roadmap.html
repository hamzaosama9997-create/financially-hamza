Step 1 of 7 ¬∑ Foundation
Understand Your Cost Structure
Before you set a single price, you need to know exactly what it costs you to deliver. Everything else is built on this foundation.
+
Why this comes first
Most businesses price based on what competitors charge or what feels right. That's not a strategy ‚Äî it's a guess. Before any pricing decision, you need four numbers clear in your head. If you can't answer all four, your pricing is based on assumption, not analysis.
The four foundations
Variable Costs
Costs that change directly with output or sales volume. Raw materials, commissions, delivery, transaction fees.
Changes with each unit sold
Fixed Costs
Costs that remain constant regardless of sales volume. Rent, salaried staff, software subscriptions, insurance.
Does not change with volume
Contribution Margin
Revenue minus variable costs. What each unit actually contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.
Revenue ‚àí Variable Cost
Break-Even Point
The minimum volume or revenue required to cover all costs. Below this line, every sale generates a loss.
Fixed Costs √∑ Contribution Margin
Real example
üöö Logistics company ‚Äî route pricing
A regional logistics company quoted jobs based on fuel and driver base salary only. Driver overtime sat in overheads and was never allocated per route. Every contract looked profitable on paper ‚Äî showing +¬£400 margin per delivery route. When overtime, vehicle wear, and admin were properly allocated, the actual margin was ‚àí¬£180 per route. Every single contract was losing money. The business only discovered this when they built a proper cost-per-job model.
The principle
Every cost must be allocated to the job that caused it. Overhead is not a separate problem ‚Äî it is a pricing problem you haven't seen yet.

Define Your Market Positioning
Your strategy sets the ceiling and floor of your pricing. Before you name a number, know where you stand in the market.
+
Three positions. Three price logics.
Each positioning strategy carries a different pricing logic. Mixing them creates margin problems that look impossible to solve ‚Äî because the root is strategic, not financial.

Real example
üìã Accounting firm ‚Äî wrong position, wrong fight
A mid-size accounting firm was pitching general services across every industry, losing 60% of proposals. Their response every time was to cut fees further ‚Äî racing to the bottom against smaller, cheaper firms. The problem wasn't the price. It was the position. They repositioned as specialists in construction and real estate only. Fees went up 40%. Win rate went from 40% to 71% in 8 months. Clients stopped comparing them to cheaper generalists because the comparison no longer made sense.
The principle
Strategy defines the boundaries of your pricing. Pricing cannot fix a positioning problem ‚Äî it will only make it more expensive.

Step 3 of 7 ¬∑ Demand
Measure Customer Value
Customers don't buy based on your cost. They buy based on the value they receive. The gap between the two is uncaptured margin.
+
Four things that drive willingness to pay
‚ú® Perceived Benefit
The outcome the customer believes your product delivers. Not features ‚Äî the result. Higher perceived outcome = higher ceiling.
üîÄ Available Alternatives
The more viable the alternatives, the lower the price ceiling. Fewer substitutes give you more pricing power.
‚ö° Urgency
A customer solving an urgent problem pays more than one making a discretionary purchase.
üîí Switching Cost
The higher the cost to switch to a competitor ‚Äî in time, money, or risk ‚Äî the less price-sensitive your customer is.
Real example
üíª B2B software reseller ‚Äî underpriced by 4x
A B2B software reseller priced at cost plus 20%. A client offhandedly mentioned the software saved their team 3 full days per month. At ¬£350/day per employee that's ¬£12,600/year in value delivered. The reseller was charging ¬£2,200. The gap ‚Äî ¬£10,400 per client per year ‚Äî multiplied across 40 clients was ¬£416,000 in uncaptured margin annually. They introduced outcome-based pricing tiers. Average contract value doubled within two renewal cycles.
The principle
Cost-plus pricing sets a floor. Value-based pricing defines the ceiling. The commercial opportunity lives in the space between the two.

Real example
üè¢ Real estate agency ‚Äî test before committing
A commercial real estate agency wanted to raise commission from 2% to 2.5%. Before rolling it firm-wide, they tested it in one city for 90 days. They lost 2 clients, kept 11. Net revenue was up 22%. Step 5 of 7 ¬∑ Control
Control Discount Governance
Discounts given without structure are margin given away without return. Governance makes discounting a tool ‚Äî not a habit.
+
The most common pricing failure isn't the list price ‚Äî it's the discount
Sales teams under pressure discount to close. Without approval thresholds or ROI requirements, the pricing strategy built in Steps 1‚Äì4 gets eroded one deal at a time. Every uncontrolled discount is margin leaving the business with no commercial justification.
Three rules that protect margin
Rule 1 ‚Äî Approval Thresholds
Define who can approve what level of discount. Sales rep, manager, CFO. Each level has a ceiling. No exceptions.
Rule 2 ‚Äî Volume Justification
A discount must be tied to a volume commitment or contract length. No volume guarantee ‚Äî no discount.
Rule 3 ‚Äî ROI Requirement
The discount must generate a measurable return: retention, upsell, referral, or strategic value.
Rule 4 ‚Äî Margin Floor
Every deal must remain above the contribution margin floor regardless of discount level. Non-negotiable.
Real example
üè≠ Manufacturing supplier ‚Äî 9 margin points lost in one quarter
Sales reps were discounting 15‚Äì25% to close deals. No approval process. No volume requirement. No floor. Gross margin dropped 9 points in one quarter before the CFO noticed. They introduced a 3-tier approval system. Margin recovered 8 points in the following quarter. Zero deals were lost due to the new policy ‚Äî volume held completely steady.
The principle
A discount without a rule is just margin leaving the business. Governance doesn't kill deals ‚Äî it protects the ones worth keeping.

Step 6 of 7 ¬∑ Discipline
Monitor & Adjust
Pricing is not a one-time decision. Markets shift, costs change, and customer behaviour evolves. FP&A must track the signals and act on them.
+
Four signals to track every reporting cycle
üìä Margin by Product
Is each product still delivering its target contribution margin? Erosion here is the first warning sign. Flag if margin drops more than 2%.
‚ö° Sales Velocity
Is volume accelerating or slowing at the current price? Unexpected velocity changes indicate price is out of alignment with market.
üö™ Customer Churn
Price-driven churn is a signal that perceived value no longer justifies the cost. Track by segment, not just total.
üèÅ Competitive Response
Are competitors repricing? A competitor's price move changes your relative positioning even when you don't move at all.
Real example
üë• Staffing agency ‚Äî revenue up, profit nowhere
The agency tracked total revenue and pooled costs. Every client looked fine. Then the finance director built a margin-by-client report for the first time ‚Äî three years into operation. One client was 30% of revenue and 4% contribution margin. Two smaller clients were 32% of revenue and 29% average margin. They repriced Client A with a 22% rate increase backed by cost data. Client accepted 60%. The agency exited low-margin volume. Revenue fell 8%. Profit grew 19%.
The principle
Adjustment is part of the discipline. A pricing strategy that never gets reviewed is not a strategy ‚Äî it is an assumption left running on autopilot.

Step 7 of 7 ¬∑ Final Level
Align Pricing with Capital Allocation
This is where pricing becomes a strategic finance decision. Pricing power determines where capital should ‚Äî and shouldn't ‚Äî be invested.
+
Three financial outcomes driven by pricing power
üíµ Free Cash Flow
Strong pricing margins generate operating cash flow that funds growth without external financing.
üìà Return on Invested Capital
Margin compression lowers ROIC. Pricing discipline protects the return that justifies capital deployment.
üèóÔ∏è Expansion Decisions
Products or markets with weak pricing power should receive less capital ‚Äî or none at all.
üéØ The Rule
Capital should follow pricing power. Reinvesting in a business that cannot defend its margin compounds the structural problem.
Real example
üè™ Retail chain ‚Äî six branches, two worth backing
A retail chain with 6 branches was splitting capex equally across all of them. Two city centre branches had 28% contribution margin. Four suburban branches had 6% ‚Äî with constant discounting needed to hold volume. The board redirected 80% of the capex budget to the two strong branches. One weak branch was closed within 18 months. Overall group margin improved from 11% to 19% over two years. The capital decision became obvious once they followed the pricing power, not the revenue.
The principle
Capital should follow pricing power ‚Äî not revenue. Where you cannot defend a price, you cannot justify reinvestment.
