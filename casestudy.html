Part 1 ‚Äî The Situation

Growing revenue. Nobody tracking margin.

The agency had grown revenue for three consecutive years. On paper, the business looked healthy. What nobody had ever done was break margin down by client. Every client appeared profitable because overhead was pooled. The monthly management accounts showed total revenue, total costs, and a bottom line that looked acceptable.
Nobody had ever asked: which clients are actually making us money ‚Äî and which ones are consuming capacity without delivering margin in return?

Client A was consuming 30% of team capacity to deliver 4% margin. Two smaller clients were generating nearly 8x more profit per pound of revenue. The business had been protecting the wrong client.
Part 3 ‚Äî The Decision

Three decisions made in one week.

1
Client A ‚Äî Go back with a revised rate
Calculated the true cost of servicing Client A including account management time, admin, and placement complexity. Presented a 22% rate increase with full cost justification.
2
Client B ‚Äî Set a margin floor and hold the line
Given 60 days to accept revised terms. Minimum 15% contribution margin floor. If not met, the relationship would not be renewed.
3
Internal ‚Äî Make margin-by-client reporting permanent
Added to monthly management accounts. No client relationship renewed without a margin review attached going forward.

You cannot manage margin you're not measuring. Three years of monthly revenue reports missed what one margin-by-client report found in an afternoon.
Big clients are not always good clients. Revenue share is not the same as profit contribution.
Repricing with data is a different conversation. When you show a client the cost justification, it stops being a negotiation and becomes a fact.
The lesson
They didn't grow their way out of the problem. They measured their way out of it. That's the difference between managing revenue and managing a business.
